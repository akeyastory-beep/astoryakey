The Interpretation of Murder

Jed Rubenfeld · 2006

United States Historical Fiction Psychological Thriller 464 pages haunting

Freud lands in New York, a girl is found strangled in a penthouse, and somehow the cure for murder turns out to be talking about your mother.

There is something genuinely exciting about the premise here: Sigmund Freud, on his one and only visit to America in the summer of 1909, gets pulled into a murder investigation involving a strangled debutante and a second victim who has lost her voice entirely, hysteria sealing her memories shut like a locked room. Jed Rubenfeld, who wrote his Princeton thesis on Freud and studied Shakespeare at Juilliard, is clearly someone who has lived with these ideas for a long time. The novel knows its history and wears it well. Early twentieth-century New York feels genuinely lived-in, the skyscrapers going up, the class divisions sharpening, the smell of ambition and rot mixed together in the summer heat.

The mystery never quite lives up to the psychoanalysis, and the psychoanalysis never quite escapes the classroom. But the city, and the era, those are real.

What the book is really about, under the murder plot, is the question of what the mind does with things it cannot bear to know. Nora Acton, the surviving victim, is treated through psychoanalysis as much as police work, and the scenes where Younger tries to coax her memories back are genuinely interesting. There is also a whole layer about Hamlet, about the famous "to be or not to be" speech, which Rubenfeld reads as a disguised confession about Oedipal desire. Whether you find this illuminating or borderline absurd probably tells you something about your relationship with Freudian theory.

The thing that stayed with me most was not the murder itself but the rivalry between Freud and Jung. They are travelling together, ostensibly allies, and the whole time there is this low current of competition and resentment running underneath their conversations. Jung is brilliant and wants to be more brilliant than his teacher. Freud is brilliant and cannot quite let go of being the one who matters most. It's almost funnier than it's meant to be, and I don't think that's entirely a flaw.

The weaknesses are real, though. The novel cannot quite decide who its central character is. Younger narrates, but Freud is the famous name. Nora is the most interesting person in the book and gets the least interiority. The pacing drags in the middle, and some of the historical set-dressing starts to feel like homework. If you can sit with a thriller that is also part lecture, it rewards patience. If you need the plot to move, it will occasionally test you.

Who should read this

Read this if you have ever found yourself genuinely curious about what Freud actually believed, or if you love historical fiction that takes its period seriously rather than using it as wallpaper. It's good for people who enjoy ideas in their thrillers and don't need the plot to be airtight. Don't pick it up if you want something pacey and propulsive, or if the phrase "Oedipal interpretation of Hamlet" makes you want to lie down in a dark room.

I
Repression and Memory
Nora's amnesia is not just a plot device, it's the whole argument of the book. What we cannot consciously hold, we bury, and what we bury keeps moving. The investigation is essentially an act of forced remembering, with all the violence that implies.
II
The Mentor and the Rival
Freud and Jung's relationship runs like a fault line beneath the whole novel. Rubenfeld is interested in what happens when admiration curdles into competition, and whether you can ever fully separate a theory from the ego of the person who invented it.
III
Old World Meets New
Freud's discomfort in America is not incidental. The novel is partly about the collision between European psychoanalytic tradition and American pragmatism, between the city's old money and its new skyscrapers, between two ideas of what modernity should look like.
IV
Desire and Violence
The killer targets women in a very specific way, and the book refuses to let that be simple. Rubenfeld uses the crimes to ask uncomfortable questions about how desire and destruction can share the same source, drawing heavily on Freud's case studies.
V
Hamlet and the Unconscious
The novel's most unusual thread: Rubenfeld's argument that Shakespeare's most famous speech is a disguised expression of Oedipal guilt. It's a wild claim and the book commits to it fully, using it as a key that unlocks both the literary mystery and the crime.

Unhappiness is caused when we cannot let go of our memories.

Why this line: It sounds simple and it is, but placed in the context of a novel about a girl whose memories have been taken from her, it becomes something stranger. The cure and the wound are the same thing.

The question is never whether a man is guilty of desire. The question is what he does with it.

Why this line: This is the moral spine of the whole book, really. Rubenfeld is not interested in innocence. He is interested in the gap between impulse and action, and what lives in that gap.

America is a place where everyone believes they have invented themselves. But no one invents themselves. We are all translated from something older.

Why this line: Freud's diagnosis of America, which he hated, given gently. It also doubles as the novel's quiet argument about psychoanalysis itself: that the past is always the text you are reading even when you think you are living in the present.

There is no greater loneliness than knowing yourself and finding no one who will believe you.

Why this line: This is Nora's situation exactly, and it's the reason the book is more interesting than its murder plot suggests. She knows something her body won't let her say.

Stratham Younger

Narrator / Protagonist

Younger is the kind of man who has found a doctrine to explain himself to himself and has not yet had to test it against something that doctrine cannot contain. He admires Freud deeply, maybe too deeply for his own judgment to work properly. What's interesting is watching him slowly pull away, not from the theory but from the blind faith in the man. He is earnest in a way that can be slow to read but is ultimately the most human thing in the book.

Sigmund Freud

Historical Figure / Mentor

Rubenfeld does something smart with Freud: he lets him be right and petty at the same time. Freud's ideas often illuminate the case, but his motives are never entirely clean. He is not particularly warm. He finds America unsophisticated and doesn't try very hard to hide it. As a fictional character he works best when the novel stops revering him and lets him be a person with an ego.

Nora Acton

Victim / Survivor

The most interesting character in the novel and, frustratingly, the one we see least clearly from the inside. She is described as rebellious, as someone who has resisted the role her family has prepared for her, and her amnesia becomes a kind of enforced obedience to the past. The book is about recovering her voice, but it might have been stronger if it had given her more of it from the start.

Carl Jung

Freud's Protege / Rival

Jung functions almost as a shadow in the novel, the version of Freud that wants to be Freud. His scenes with the older man have the charged atmosphere of a relationship where both parties know the break is coming but are still pretending it isn't. Rubenfeld doesn't over-explain this, which is where he is wisest. He lets the rivalry speak through small moments of professional condescension and barely-contained ambition.

Things in this book that most people miss on a first read. Click to reveal.

Rubenfeld didn't invent Freud's contempt for America. After his real 1909 visit to Clark University, Freud reportedly said he had brought the plague to America, meaning psychoanalysis. He found Americans too cheerful, too uninterested in the unconscious, too convinced that the past could simply be moved beyond. He blamed the trip for a decline in his health. The novel captures this almost too faithfully: Freud's dismissiveness functions both as character detail and as a kind of historical revenge for the country that would later misuse his ideas most aggressively.

Rubenfeld's central literary argument is that Hamlet's "to be or not to be" speech is not really about suicide but about whether to act against a parent who has done something unforgivable. In the novel, this reading becomes a key to understanding both the killer's psychology and Nora's situation. She cannot speak because speaking means naming something her mind has decided she cannot survive knowing. The Hamlet layer is not decoration: it's the interpretive framework the whole mystery depends on. Most readers skim it and then wonder why the ending feels like it came out of nowhere.

The bridge was under construction in 1909, with labourers digging through the silt at the bottom of the East River to lay foundations. Rubenfeld uses it as a recurring image of the unconscious: the vast, dark, pressurised space beneath the surface where the real structural work is happening, invisible to everyone above. When the novel goes literally underground in its later sections, it's not just geography. It's the same metaphor the whole book has been building.

Rubenfeld wrote his undergraduate thesis on Freud at Princeton and studied Shakespearean acting at Juilliard before going to Harvard Law. The novel's Hamlet interpretation is drawn from his own academic work, not invented for the book. Several of the psychoanalytic case studies referenced in the story are real Freud cases with details borrowed from the original texts. What Rubenfeld invented is the connecting thread: the idea that the same psychological structure visible in Hamlet is also visible in the killer. The academic ambition is genuine. Whether it fully pays off in the novel is where readers tend to disagree.

Harry Thaw shot and killed architect Stanford White in 1906 in one of the most sensational murders in American history, motivated by his obsession with showgirl Evelyn Nesbit, whom White had previously seduced. Thaw was sent to Matteawan State Hospital for the Criminally Insane rather than executed, because his lawyers successfully argued temporary insanity. In the novel, the possibility that he has escaped to commit further crimes is a genuine red herring that the book uses to comment on how American society handles dangerous wealthy men: very carefully, and with considerable paperwork.
