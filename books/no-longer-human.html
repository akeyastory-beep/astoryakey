Book review

No Longer Human

Osamu Dazai · Japan · 1948

Aashima's rating ★★★★★
Mood Empty · Existential
Read in 2024
Pages 177
Japanese Literature Confessional Existential Semi-autobiographical Dazai

There is a specific kind of loneliness that does not come from being alone — it comes from being surrounded by people and still feeling like you are performing. Like everyone else received a manual for being human and yours got lost somewhere. Osamu Dazai wrote No Longer Human about that feeling, and he did it so precisely that reading it feels less like reading and more like being found out.

The novel follows Yozo Oba, told through three notebooks he leaves behind. From childhood, Yozo has understood that he cannot understand people. He compensates by becoming a performer — funny, agreeable, self-effacing — while inside he experiences something closer to dread. The book is about what happens to a person who spends their entire life pretending to be something they don't know how to be.

"Mine has been a life of much shame. I can't even guess myself what it must be to live the life of a human being."

What makes Dazai so devastating is that Yozo is not unlikable. He is desperately, achingly human — which is exactly the irony the title is working with. The very intensity of his feeling is what makes him feel like an alien. He loves people too much and understands them too little and cannot reconcile these two things, so he drinks and he drifts and he disappears into women who temporarily make him feel less alone.

Dazai finished this novel weeks before his death. Knowing that changes everything and nothing — the book stands without biography, but knowing Yozo is Dazai gives it a weight that sits in your chest for days. This is not a comfortable book. It is the kind of book that knows your secrets. I read it in one sitting and then stared at the ceiling for a while.

Read it if you have ever felt like you were watching human life from slightly outside of it. Read it if you have ever been the funniest person in a room and also the most lost. Read it if you can handle a book that will not offer you a resolution, only recognition.

01
The performance of humanity
Yozo spends his entire life acting — performing joy, performing normalcy, performing whatever the room seems to require. Dazai is asking a brutal question: if you perform being human long enough, does it ever become real? Or are you just exhausted?
"I kept performing the role of an eccentric to make them laugh."
02
Shame as identity
The novel opens with shame and never leaves it. But Dazai's shame is not simple guilt — it is a total alienation from the self, a sense that one's own existence is an embarrassment. This is the most precise literary account of that specific feeling I have encountered.
"Mine has been a life of much shame."
03
The warmth of self-destruction
Alcohol, women, and later morphine — Yozo's addictions are not escape so much as warmth. In a world where he cannot connect, substances offer the closest thing to feeling. Dazai is not moralising. He is reporting.
"Drinking was the only way I could talk to people without terror."
04
Women as temporary shelter
Every woman in Yozo's life represents a different kind of shelter — and each one he destroys or abandons or is simply too broken to hold onto. Dazai doesn't glamorise this. The women are vivid and real and Yozo's damage ripples through all of them.
"Women have always taken care of me, but I have been incapable of being saved."
05
The unreliable self-narrator
Yozo tells his own story but cannot be trusted — not because he lies, but because he genuinely cannot see himself clearly. This is the novel's most sophisticated structural choice: we are reading a confession that confesses to the impossibility of confession.
"I am not sure whether I was born a coward or whether I became one."
06
Art as the only honest language
Yozo is a painter and cartoonist — the only spaces where he doesn't have to perform. Dazai suggests that art is where the self can be true precisely because it is not life. The notebooks themselves are Yozo's most honest act.
"When I draw, I do not lie."
Y
Yozo Oba
Protagonist · Narrator
Yozo is the author of the three notebooks — and Dazai's most direct self-portrait. A man of immense sensitivity and almost no self-preservation, he moves through life performing versions of himself while the real self quietly dissolves. He is funny, charming, and utterly lost. He is the kind of person who makes everyone else feel seen while remaining invisible himself.
→ Aashima's note: Yozo reminded me of the way some people use humour as armour. The joke is always the wall.
T
Takeichi
Childhood peer · Catalyst
The boy who sees through Yozo's performance as a child and tells him so. This single moment of being truly seen — rather than performing for — haunts Yozo for the rest of his life. Takeichi understands, in his strange way, that Yozo's clowning is deliberate. This terrifies and fascinates Yozo in equal measure.
→ Aashima's note: Takeichi appears briefly but he changes everything. Being seen without performing for the first time is either liberation or catastrophe.
Y
Yoshiko
Wife · The innocent
Yozo's wife — pure, trusting, and the most devastating presence in the novel. Her innocence is not naivety; it is a genuine goodness that Yozo cannot comprehend or protect. What happens to Yoshiko is the novel's moral centre, and the event from which Yozo never recovers.
→ Aashima's note: Yoshiko is heartbreaking because she trusts completely. In Dazai's world, that is almost a death sentence.
H
Horiki
Companion · Corruptor
Yozo's most consistent companion — a painter, a drinker, a man who enables every self-destructive impulse Yozo has. But Dazai is careful not to make Horiki a villain. He is simply someone who has made peace with dissipation, and Yozo has not. Their friendship is a slow-motion mirror Yozo cannot look away from.
→ Aashima's note: Horiki is the person you keep around because they make your worst choices feel normal.
Mine has been a life of much shame. I can't even guess myself what it must be to live the life of a human being.
The opening sentence. Everything that follows is the explanation of this.
I have always shook with fright before human beings. Unable as I was to feel the least particle of confidence in my ability to speak and act like a human being, I kept my solitary agonies locked in my breast.
The anxiety underneath the performance — the state Yozo lives in permanently.
Now I have neither happiness nor unhappiness. Everything passes. That is the one and only thing I have thought resembled a truth in the society of human beings where I have dwelled up till now.
Yozo near the end. This is either wisdom or surrender — Dazai leaves that ambiguous.
I thought that if I could make people laugh, it would be all right even if I were different from other people.
Yozo's strategy, articulated simply. The joke as the social contract.
What I felt was not grief nor loneliness, but a quiet amazement. To think that human beings could be deceived so easily.
Yozo watching his own performance work. The horror underneath the relief.
He was a good boy. Please tell that to God.
The final lines, spoken by the woman who knew Yozo. These ten words are the entire tragedy of the novel.

The last notebook

Osamu Dazai completed No Longer Human in May 1948. On June 13, 1948, he drowned in the Tamagawa Canal in Tokyo with his companion Tomie Yamazaki — a double suicide. The novel was published posthumously that same year.

Knowing this does not make the book more tragic — the tragedy was already total. But it does change what the final lines mean. "He was a good boy" is no longer just Yozo's epitaph. It is Dazai's. The notebooks were always a goodbye.
