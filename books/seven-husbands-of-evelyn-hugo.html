The Seven Husbands of Evelyn Hugo

Taylor Jenkins Reid · 2017

United States Historical Fiction 400 pages nostalgic

She was never real, and yet I grieved her — that's the kind of character Taylor Jenkins Reid made Evelyn Hugo.

Picture Elizabeth Taylor's allure, the boldness of Katharine Hepburn, the fire of Sophia Loren, and the glamour of Marilyn Monroe — all woven into one unforgettable character. That's Evelyn Hugo for you. Taylor Jenkins Reid introduces a woman so vividly drawn that by the last page, I was tempted to Google her filmography, only to remember she's entirely fictional. Reid doesn't just create Evelyn; she crafts an entire Hollywood world filled with dazzling moments, raw emotions, and the intricate tangle of personal and public lives that come with stardom.

She was never real, and yet I grieved her. That's the kind of writer Taylor Jenkins Reid is.

This book isn't just Evelyn's glamorous journey. It's a universal story about resilience, the price of ambition, and staying true to yourself when the world keeps asking you to be something smaller. Evelyn's life unfolds through her marriages, each one a new chapter filled with sacrifice, betrayal, and regret. But beneath all the Hollywood gloss, it's really about the love she couldn't name in public, the identity she had to hide to survive, and the cost of building an empire on secrets. Reid understands something most writers don't: that the most devastating losses aren't always the ones people know about.

Harry Cameron is, hands down, the heart of this book. Not because of romance but because of what Reid does with their friendship, this soulmate-in-everything-but-name dynamic that the novel earns completely. What stayed with me long after I finished was a specific kind of ache: for Celia and Evelyn, for the life they could have had in a different decade, in a different world. There's a scene near the end that I won't spoil, but I sat with it for three days. Some books do that. They leave a bruise.

And then there's Monique. Her storyline runs parallel to Evelyn's confessions, and the connection between them, when it's finally revealed, is the kind of twist that reframes everything you thought you understood. It's not a trick. It's earned. Reid plants the seeds so carefully that looking back, you can see every one of them. This is the kind of storytelling that makes you want to start over from page one the minute you close it.

More than a story about Old Hollywood, this book is about living unapologetically on your own terms, even when the terms are cruel, even when you hurt people you love, even when you have to reinvent yourself so many times you're not sure who you started as. Evelyn's advice to Monique near the end of the book is the kind of thing you write on a sticky note and put on your mirror. Read this. It will stay with you.

Who should read this

Read this if you've ever had to be one version of yourself in public and a completely different one in private. If you love Old Hollywood, big feelings, bisexual love stories, and a twist that actually earns its weight. Read this if you want to cry on the last fifty pages and feel good about it. Don't read this if you need your protagonist to be likeable in a clean, uncomplicated way. Evelyn Hugo is not that, and that's exactly the point.

I
The Cost of Fame
Evelyn's entire life is an extended negotiation with fame, trading pieces of herself for survival and power. Reid shows that for women, especially women of colour, fame in mid-century Hollywood was less a gift than a transaction, and the terms were always unfair.
II
Identity and Reinvention
Evelyn Herrera becomes Evelyn Hugo: she bleaches her hair, flattens her accent, erases her Cuban roots. The novel is obsessed with the question of how much self you can give away before there's nothing left to find. The answer, Reid suggests, is more complicated than you'd expect.
III
Forbidden Love
Evelyn and Celia's relationship is the emotional spine of the book. Set against a backdrop of homophobia and social pressure, their love story is not triumphant in any simple sense. It's painful, real, and tender in a way that romance between unmarked characters rarely has to be.
IV
Ambition vs Morality
Evelyn makes choices throughout her life that are hard to defend and even harder to condemn. Reid puts the reader constantly in the uncomfortable position of understanding exactly why someone does something wrong. That moral complexity is what separates this from an ordinary rags-to-riches story.
V
Truth and Legacy
Why does Evelyn choose to tell the truth now, at 79, through a journalist she's never met? The novel circles this question obsessively. Reid suggests that truth isn't something you arrive at, it's something you choose, and sometimes the choosing comes very, very late.

People think that intimacy is about sex. But intimacy is about truth. When you realize you can tell someone your truth, when you can show yourself to them, when you stand in front of them bare and their response is "you're safe with me" — that's intimacy.

Why this line: This is the sentence that unlocks the whole book. Every marriage Evelyn enters is examined against this definition, and most of them fail it. The one relationship that passes the test isn't any of the seven husbands.

They are just husbands. I am Evelyn Hugo. And anyway, I think once people know the truth, they will be much more interested in my wife.

Why this line: The book builds to this moment for 400 pages. That last sentence lands like a door swinging open after being locked for decades. It's triumphant and heartbreaking at the same time.

The difference between a star and an actress is that the star feels comfortable being the very thing the world wants her to be.

Why this line: This is Reid's sharpest observation about the machine of Hollywood and the cost of living inside it. Evelyn chose to be a star, and the novel shows exactly what that choice meant for everything else she wanted.

When you're given an opportunity to change your life, be ready to do whatever it takes to make it happen.

Why this line: Early in the book this reads as simple ambition. By the end, after you've seen what "whatever it takes" actually cost Evelyn, the line takes on a whole different weight. It becomes a warning as much as advice.

Evelyn Hugo

Protagonist

A woman who has spent her entire life making calculated moves and then living with the consequences of them. What makes Evelyn so riveting is that she's not a victim and she's not a villain. She is someone who understood the game, played it better than anyone, and still lost the things that mattered most. Her psychology is built around a central terror: being sent back to the poverty and powerlessness she started with. Everything she does, including the cruelties, makes sense through that lens.

Celia St. James

Evelyn's great love

Where Evelyn is armour, Celia is nerve endings. She is openly herself in ways that Evelyn never allows herself to be, and that difference is at the core of their tragedy. Celia wants to be loved publicly; Evelyn can't afford to give that. The resentment that builds from that gap is one of the most honest portrayals of how two people can love each other completely and still not be able to make it work.

Harry Cameron

Husband and best friend

The emotional anchor of the whole novel. Harry is the person Evelyn can actually be herself with, and Reid makes their friendship the most quietly devastating relationship in a book full of loud ones. His gentleness exists alongside his own secrets, and the way those secrets connect to Evelyn's is one of the more tender plot revelations the book offers.

Monique Grant

Narrator and biographer

Monique starts out as a vessel for the reader's curiosity and ends up becoming the point of the whole story. Her parallel journey, dealing with a separation and a career she's not sure she deserves, mirrors Evelyn's in ways that only become clear at the end. She's less flashy than Evelyn but her growth over the course of the book is real and earned.

Don Adler

Second husband

The most straightforwardly villainous character in the book, but Reid still gives him a logic. Don is what happens when a man's sense of identity is entirely constructed around status and control, and those things are threatened by a woman who refuses to be smaller than him. His abuse of Evelyn is shown without flinching and without melodrama, which makes it more disturbing than any dramatic treatment could.

Sunset Boulevard

Billy Wilder · 1950

The definitive portrait of Old Hollywood consuming its own, told from the inside with the same mix of glamour and rot that Reid captures in Evelyn's story.

Carol

Todd Haynes · 2015

A love story between two women in 1950s America, shot in muted gorgeous light, about desire that can't be spoken out loud. Feels like Evelyn and Celia's chapters brought to screen.

Feud: Bette and Joan

Ryan Murphy · 2017

The exact era and world, real Hollywood women navigating an industry that wanted to use them and then discard them. The texture of Reid's novel lives in this series.

Listen while you read...

Playlist mood Old Hollywood strings slowly giving way to something lonelier. Lush orchestra at first — think Bernard Herrmann or the score from All About Eve. Then, as Evelyn's story gets darker, shift to sparse piano, minor keys, music that sounds like memory and regret. Try Jonny Greenwood's Spencer soundtrack, or anything from Nils Frahm's Solo album. Late night, low light. That's the vibe.

If this book made you think, explore...

The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life

Erving Goffman · Sociology

Goffman's theory that all social interaction is performance maps exactly onto Evelyn's public persona. She is, essentially, the most sophisticated example of impression management ever put on the page.

Compulsory Heterosexuality

Adrienne Rich · Feminist Theory

Rich's essay about heterosexuality as an enforced institution rather than a natural state illuminates exactly the pressure Evelyn and Celia are living under throughout the novel.

Things in this book that most people miss on a first read. Click to reveal.

Reid names each section with a descriptor attached to the husband: "Poor Ernie Diaz," "Goddamn Don Adler," "Brilliant, kindhearted, tortured Harry Cameron." On a first read, these feel like character summaries. On a reread you realise they're almost entirely Evelyn's verdict on herself within that marriage, reflecting her emotional state and self-awareness at each stage of her life. The adjectives shift from resigned to angry to tender in ways that track Evelyn's growth more than the men's.

Most readers assume Evelyn is Elizabeth Taylor (eight marriages to seven men, enormous fame, epic on-screen presence). But Reid also drew heavily from Rita Hayworth, who similarly changed her name, dyed her hair, and concealed her Hispanic heritage to succeed in Hollywood. Ava Gardner's habit of giving blunt, unfiltered interviews to a journalist she trusted is also baked into the book's entire structure. Evelyn is a composite, and recognising that makes the novel feel less like fiction and more like a documentary told sideways.

Evelyn tells Monique early on that she read an article Monique wrote about assisted suicide. This seems like a throwaway detail about how Evelyn found her. It's not. That article is the reason. Evelyn is planning to die on her own terms, she has already made that decision, and she wanted a biographer who understood, without flinching, the human capacity to choose the conditions of your own ending. Everything Monique thinks is mysterious about being chosen is actually sitting right there in the first twenty pages.

Mick Riva, one of Evelyn's husbands, is also the absent, negligent father at the centre of Malibu Rising. Reading both books together reframes Mick completely. In Evelyn Hugo, he's a minor character, more plot device than person. In Malibu Rising, you see the wreckage of exactly the kind of self-serving charm Evelyn saw through immediately. Reid is building a universe where the same people orbit each other across decades, and the cameos reward re-readers who pay attention.

Evelyn chooses to sell twelve gowns for a breast cancer charity in honour of her daughter. The gowns were worn at specific public moments throughout her career. On a close read, each piece of clothing corresponds to a period of her life marked by a particular relationship or loss. The auction isn't nostalgia. It's Evelyn curating the physical evidence of who she was, deciding what to leave behind and what the world is allowed to know. The clothes are a second autobiography running underneath the spoken one.
